Astroworld and the Twitter Conspiracy Theory Rabbit-Hole

By Bex Sander, 17/01/2022

Photo by Alexander Londoño on Unsplash

Astroworld Festival was an annual music festival run by American rapper Travis Scott, held in Houston, Texas, at NRG Park, near the former site of Six Flags AstroWorld. The festival was first held in November 2018.

The 2020 festival was canceled due to the COVID-19 pandemic in Texas. Astroworld Festival 2021 was originally scheduled for November 5–6, 2021. On the first night, a crowd crush occurred, resulting in the deaths of at least ten people and the cancellation of the second night of the festival. Lawsuits against Travis Scott and the companies involved in producing the festival are ongoing, as well as a Congressional investigation.

In the aftermath of the tragedy, whilst the damage was being assessed, conspiracy theories flourished. In particular, accusatiions of the crowd crush being an intentionally orchestrated event in order to perform some kind of ritual human sacrifice swept social media, including Twitter and Tiktok.

Between the 5th and the 12th of November there were 81,034 tweets about Astroworld. Within that, 1,629 tweets included the word “satanic” or “ritual”. This only accounts for 2% of the overall tweets. This is roughly the same as the top tweeter about Astroworld, Apple Music, who sponsored and live-streamed the event until it was shut down. The overall tweet rate for the time in question works out to roughly 422 tweets per hour, which has a spike on the 11th November. The distribution of tweets per day is fairly regular, aside from the 5th November (the day before the festival began proper) all days have a tweet rate between 100,000 and 120,000 per day. The tweets references a satanic conspiracy theory have a much more obvious trend that spikes on the 9th November with 468 tweets that day, 28% of the overall tweets.

71% of tweets were retweets

There was a lot of bot activity mentioning Astroworld, 45% of the top tweeters were bots. There wasn't much bot activity in the satanic discourse, which makes sense with it being a much more specific topic and was not considered newsworthy until the tweet traffic had already built up. What is interesting to note is that the account that made the most unique tweets about astroworld was less than a year old and only contained tweets about astroworld. The uniqueness of each tweet doesn’t suggest bot behaviour, more likely it is what is known as a “sock puppet” account. Accounts like this can be as malicious and deceptive as bots. However I chose not to remove them from my overall analysis because the accounts were created specifically to comment on the Astroworld incident, and because they are most likely created by individuals they are relevant.

    There were high retweet ratios across the board, with 71% of overall tweets being retweets, whereas within the satanic conspiracy subset the retweet rate was 66%. Academics argue that retweets have a sense of extra validity as they cite another source for the information, but the consensus on their trustworthiness is debatable. It is interesting to note that the most retweeted tweets that reference anything satanic or ritual are actually deriding the conspiracy theory rather than spreading misinformation. This does make sense as conspiracy theories are in their nature fringe beleifs, so the conensus on Twitter is that it isn't true. However as we have learnt from the last few years fringe beleifs can still do major damage when they are furtively held.
